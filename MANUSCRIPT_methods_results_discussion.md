# Genome-Resolved Hybrid Metagenomics of the CAN Enhanced Biological Phosphorus Removal Bioreactor Community: Recovery of 276 MAGs and Inference of Nitrogen- and Phosphorus-Cycling Potential

## 1. Introduction

The "CAN" study comprised five shotgun metagenome samples (designated CAN_1 through CAN_5) drawn from an engineered enhanced biological phosphorus removal (EBPR) bioreactor community of relevance to nitrogen cycling (denitrification and dissimilatory nitrate reduction to ammonium, DNRA) and phosphorus cycling (polyphosphate accumulation by polyphosphate-accumulating organisms, PAOs, and the competing glycogen-accumulating organisms, GAOs). Each sample was sequenced with both Oxford Nanopore long reads and Illumina paired-end short reads, and a parallel 16S rRNA gene amplicon time-series was generated to provide a longitudinal community context. The objectives were to recover high-quality metagenome-assembled genomes (MAGs) by hybrid long-plus-short-read assembly, to annotate nitrogen- and phosphorus-cycling genes within those MAGs, to link 16S amplicon sequence variants (ASVs) to MAG-borne 16S genes, and to quantify MAG and gene abundance across the five metagenomes and across the amplicon time-series.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Sequencing data and read quality control

Each of the five samples was sequenced with both Oxford Nanopore long reads and Illumina paired-end short reads (file naming `CAN_n_S97..S101_R1/R2_001.fastq.gz`), and a parallel DADA2-processed 16S rRNA gene amplicon time-series spanning 75 sampling days was generated. Details of the wet-lab procedures — DNA extraction protocol, library preparation chemistry, Nanopore flow-cell/basecaller configuration beyond the polishing model, and Illumina instrument run parameters — were not recorded in the available pipeline outputs and could not be reconstructed. Two inferences are noted: the Nanopore basecaller chemistry is implied by the Medaka consensus-polishing model (`r1041_e82_400bps_sup_v5.0.0`; see §2.2), and the Illumina library was generated on a two-colour-chemistry instrument (NovaSeq/NextSeq-class), inferred from the automatic detection of poly-G artefacts during quality control; fastp cannot distinguish NovaSeq from NextSeq, so the specific instrument model was not determined.

Illumina reads were quality-controlled with fastp v1.3.3 using the flags `--detect_adapter_for_pe --correction --cut_tail --cut_tail_window_size 4 --cut_tail_mean_quality 20 --length_required 50`, executed with 16 threads (`config["threads_qc"] = 16`). Poly-G trimming was enabled automatically on the basis of two-colour-chemistry header detection. Deduplication (`--dedup`) and low-complexity filtering (`--low_complexity_filter`) were deliberately not applied, in order to preserve low-abundance taxa and AT-rich genomes. Per-sample read yields before and after filtering, post-filter base counts, and post-filter Q30 fractions (from each `qc/<sample>/fastp.json`) are summarised in Table 1. Read counts before filtering were CAN_1 158,924,368; CAN_2 204,171,134; CAN_3 186,684,912; CAN_4 192,873,320; and CAN_5 164,662,770. After filtering, the five samples yielded between 156.0 and 201.0 million reads (CAN_1 156,014,214; CAN_2 201,000,058; CAN_3 184,647,582; CAN_4 189,606,110; CAN_5 162,050,316), corresponding to 22.47–29.00 Gbp of cleaned sequence with Q30 fractions of 95.75–96.23% (per-sample 95.99/96.09/95.75/96.23/96.05% for CAN_1–CAN_5). Cleaned reads were written to `reads/illumina/<sample>_R{1,2}.clean.fq.gz`.

Nanopore long reads were filtered with Filtlong v0.3.1 using `--min_length 1000 --keep_percent 95` (configured as `filtlong_min_length: 1000`, `filtlong_keep_percent: 95`) and recompressed with pigz; long-read quality was profiled (but not gated) with NanoPlot v1.46.2 (`--fastq --threads 8`). Post-filtering long-read totals, read N50 and read N90 (read from the Flye logs that consumed the filtlong output) are listed in Table 1: per-sample totals were CAN_1 22.51 Gbp, CAN_2 18.07 Gbp, CAN_3 26.42 Gbp, CAN_4 27.83 Gbp, and CAN_5 26.82 Gbp, with read N50 values of 5,440/5,424/5,406/6,158/4,469 bp and read N90 values of 2,051/2,073/2,075/2,122/1,721 bp. The pooled co-assembly read set totalled 121,649,398,006 bp (read N50 5,327 bp; read N90 1,979 bp).

### 2.2 Hybrid de novo assembly and short-read assembly

The study employed two parallel assembly tracks orchestrated by a Snakemake (≥8) workflow (`/scratch/afreiburger/metag-hybrid/pipeline/Snakefile`): a long-read-anchored hybrid track (from which all downstream MAGs were derived) and a short-read-only metaSPAdes track.

**Hybrid track.** Long-read assemblies were produced with metaFlye v2.9.6-b1802 in metagenome mode (`flye --nano-hq <reads> --out-dir <01_flye> --meta --threads 64`); the `--nano-hq` read type was configured via `flye_read_type: nano-hq`. Each draft was then polished through a fixed four-step chain. First, Medaka v2.2.1 (model `r1041_e82_400bps_sup_v5.0.0`) performed consensus polishing; the rule aligned reads to the draft with `mini_align`, split contigs into two BED region groups (`scripts/split_contigs_for_medaka.py --n-groups 2`), and ran two parallel `medaka inference` processes pinned to `CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES=0` and `=1` respectively (each `--batch_size 200`, 16 threads), stitching the two HDF outputs with `medaka sequence` into a consensus FASTA. Second, short-read polishing was applied with Polypolish v0.6.1: the consensus was indexed and the cleaned Illumina R1 and R2 sets were aligned separately in all-alignment mode (`bwa mem -a`; bwa v0.7.18), filtered with `polypolish filter`, and polished with `polypolish polish`. For the co-assembly, the Polypolish filter and polish steps were split into two rules to permit re-entry, because filtering of the 1.6-Gbp co-assembly held very large memory (~1.27 TB resident) in a long-running stage. Third, a single round of Pilon v1.24 (`pilon -Xmx256g --genome <input> --frags pilon.bam --output <s> --outdir . --threads 32 --fix snps,indels --changes`) corrected residual SNPs and indels, with the input BAM produced by bwa-mem2 (v2.2.1) and samtools (v1.23.1). The Pilon outputs (`04_pilon/<sample>.fasta`, symlinked as `final.fasta`) constitute the final hybrid assemblies.

**Short-read track.** Cleaned Illumina reads were assembled in parallel with metaSPAdes (SPAdes v4.2.0, Python 3.11.15) in metagenome mode (`spades.py --meta -1 <R1.clean> -2 <R2.clean> -o <out> -t 64 -m 600 -k 21,33,55,77,99,127 --tmp-dir <local NVMe>`), with a 600-GB memory cap per sample and 1,800 GB for the co-assembly. The k-mer ladder was 21,33,55,77,99,127. Temporary files were routed to local NVMe to avoid the performance penalty of many-small-file writes on NFS, and downstream binning used `contigs.fasta` (not `scaffolds.fasta`) to avoid scaffold-breakpoint chimeras.

**Co-assembly.** Co-assembly was enabled (`do_coassembly: true`). All five samples' reads were pooled by concatenation into single read sets — Nanopore (`reads/nanopore/coassembly.filt.fastq.gz`) and Illumina (`coassembly_R1/R2.clean.fq.gz`). Although the workflow supports latitude/longitude-based co-assembly grouping, no geographic metadata existed for these samples, so the grouping logic fell back to a single pooled ("all") model; thus a single five-sample co-assembly was produced on both tracks.

### 2.3 Genome binning

Genome binning was performed with MetaBAT2 v2.18 on the hybrid assemblies only (parallel binning of the short-read metaSPAdes contigs was configured but produced zero bins and was not used). MetaBAT2 was invoked as `metabat2 -i <asm> -a <depth> -o <out>/bin -m 2500 -t <threads> --seed 1`, i.e. with a minimum contig length of 2,500 bp; the run-time parameters logged were `minContig 2500, minCV 1.0, minCVSum 1.0, maxP 95%, minS 60, maxEdges 200, minClsSize 200000, seed 1`.

Contig coverage was computed by mapping the fastp-cleaned Illumina reads to the polished hybrid assemblies with bwa-mem2 v2.2.1, sorting with samtools v1.23.1, and summarising depth with `jgi_summarize_bam_contig_depths` (distributed with MetaBAT2 2.18); Nanopore reads were not used for coverage. For the per-sample hybrid assemblies, only self-coverage (single-sample depth) was used. For the co-assembly, all five sample read-sets were mapped independently to the co-assembly contigs, producing five separate BAMs (`coverage_CAN_1.bam` … `coverage_CAN_5.bam`) that were summarised into a single depth matrix, yielding a genuine five-sample differential-coverage profile (confirmed by the `depth.txt` header carrying `coverage_CAN_1 … coverage_CAN_5` columns). Binning yielded 1,623 candidate bins prior to dereplication: 211, 177, 230, 243 and 182 bins from the five per-sample hybrid assemblies (CAN_1–CAN_5), and 580 from the co-assembly.

### 2.4 Dereplication and MAG quality assessment

Bin quality was assessed with CheckM2 v1.1.0 (`checkm2 predict --threads 32 --input bin.*.fa --output-directory <out> --database_path uniref100.KO.*.dmnd --force`). All 1,623 candidate bins were assessed; over this full pre-dereplication candidate set, completeness was predicted by the neural-network (specific) model for 1,115 bins and by the gradient-boost (general) model for the remaining 508. This model split refers to the 1,623-bin candidate set and was not tabulated per final MAG. Across the 1,623 candidate bins, CheckM2 completeness was distributed as ≥90% → 439 bins, [70,90)% → 196, [50,70)% → 113, and <50% → 875, and contamination as <5% → 1,433, [5,10)% → 78, and ≥10% → 112.

The 1,623 bins were dereplicated with dRep v3.6.2 using skani v0.3.2 as the secondary ANI backend: `dRep dereplicate <workdir> --genomes <staged>/*.fa --genomeInfo <merged CheckM2 table> --completeness 50 --contamination 10 -sa 0.95 --S_algorithm skani -p 32`. Because the pre-computed CheckM2 genomeInfo table was supplied, dRep did not re-run CheckM internally, so the CheckM2 v1.1.0 values are authoritative throughout. The quality pre-filter (completeness ≥ 50%, contamination ≤ 10%) was passed by 651 of 1,623 candidate bins (40.11%); these clustered (via 2,251 skani comparisons) into 276 secondary clusters at the 95% ANI species threshold, from which 276 winning representatives were selected — the final MAG catalogue.

MAG quality was expressed using MIMAG-proxy tiers based on CheckM2 completeness and contamination only; because CheckM2 does not evaluate rRNA (5S/16S/23S) or tRNA inventories, these tiers are completeness/contamination quality classes rather than the full MIMAG standard (which additionally requires ≥18 tRNAs and rRNA presence for the high-quality designation). Across the final 276 MAGs, completeness averaged 89.03% (median 95.44%, range 50.04–100.00%) and contamination averaged 1.63% (median 1.00%, range 0.00–8.83%); all 276 MAGs were at least medium-quality (≥50% completeness, ≤10% contamination) by construction of the dRep floor, and 162 MAGs met the stricter high-quality proxy threshold (≥90% completeness and ≤5% contamination). The 9-MAG difference between the 171 MAGs at ≥90% completeness and the 162 high-quality MAGs reflects high-completeness representatives retained at 5–10% contamination, permitted by the dRep contamination floor of 10%. All completeness, contamination, and quality-tier statistics reported in this manuscript refer to the 276 dereplicated MAGs; the 1,623 figures denote pre-dereplication candidate bins only.

### 2.5 Taxonomic classification

The 276 dereplicated MAGs were taxonomically classified with GTDB-Tk v2.7.2 (`classify_wf`; `gtdbtk classify_wf --genome_dir mag/drep/dereplicated_genomes --out_dir mag/gtdbtk --cpus 32 --extension fa`) against GTDB release r220 (deployed at `refdata/gtdbtk/release220`). Classification was performed locally rather than deferred to any external service. No archaeal placements were produced (only `gtdbtk.bac120.summary.tsv` was generated; no `gtdbtk.ar53.summary.tsv`), and no genomes failed (`gtdbtk.failed_genomes.tsv` was empty). Across the 276 MAGs, 140 were classified by topology ("fully defined by topology"), 92 by ANI screen, and 44 by relative evolutionary divergence (RED, "taxonomic novelty determined using RED"). As a caveat, GTDB-Tk v2.7.2 expects reference data release r232, whereas the deployed reference was r220; every run accordingly logged the warning "you are not using the reference data intended for this release: r232." The classifications proceeded without failures, but the resulting taxonomy corresponds to GTDB r220 and is reported as such throughout.

### 2.6 Structural and functional annotation

Three annotators were applied to the 276 MAGs. Structural and general functional annotation was performed with Bakta v1.12.0 (database v6.0, "full" type; `bakta --db <DB> --output <out> --threads 8 --skip-plot --force <MAG>.fa`, parallelised at 16 concurrent jobs). Orthology- and pathway-level annotation was performed with eggNOG-mapper v2.1.13 against eggNOG database 5.0.2 with DIAMOND v2.2.0 as the search backend; the run-of-record (the out-of-band resume script that produced the final outputs) used `emapper.py --data_dir <eggnog> -i <MAG.fa> -o <MAG> --output_dir <out> --cpu 8 --override --itype genome --genepred prodigal --dbmem` with eggNOG-mapper defaults `--sensitive --iterate` (maximum-quality mode). Of the 276 eggNOG annotation files, 9 were empty (CAN_1_bin.{2,48,174,177,180,195,210} and CAN_3_bin.{43,227}), leaving 267 non-empty files; these 9 divergent bins were nonetheless covered by the targeted HMM scan.

A targeted KO HMM scan was performed against a curated panel of 42 KOfam profiles using HMMER v3.4 `hmmsearch` (`hmmsearch --cpu 64 --tblout hmmsearch.tblout --domtblout hmmsearch.domtblout -E 1e-5 --noali target_profiles.hmm all_mags.faa`), where `all_mags.faa` was the concatenation of all 276 Bakta CDS proteomes (930,633 protein records, reconciling exactly with 930,591 Bakta CDSs + 42 sORFs), reheadered as `MAG|locus_tag`. Per-KO adaptive score thresholds were applied in post-processing (`postprocess.py`): for each KO the domain score was used when its score type was "domain" (23 KOs) and the full-sequence score otherwise (19 KOs), and hits below the KO-specific threshold were discarded.

The final per-MAG targeted KO presence/absence calls were taken as the union, per MAG, of the threshold-passing KOfam HMM hits and the eggNOG KO calls (the `KEGG_ko` field) restricted to the 42 target KOs. These calls are stored in the published presence/absence matrix `ko_presence_absence_matrix.csv` (276 × 42) and, after a subsequent regeneration that added KO and time-series columns, in the master table `mag_abundance_summary.tsv`. Because the master table was regenerated after the standalone KO matrix was written, a small number of per-KO prevalence counts differ by a few MAGs between the two snapshots; all phenotype inference (§2.9) and all per-KO prevalences reported in this manuscript were computed from the current `mag_abundance_summary.tsv`, which is the matrix that feeds the phenotype rules, and are therefore internally consistent. Where the standalone `ko_presence_absence_matrix.csv` snapshot is referenced, this is stated explicitly.

The 42-KO panel spanned eight pathways/modules: denitrification (10 KOs: K00370 narG, K00371 narH, K00374 narI, K02567 napA, K02568 napB, K15864 nirS, K00368 nirK, K04561 norB, K02305 norC, K00376 nosZ); DNRA/nitrogen-other (4 KOs: K00362 nirB, K00363 nirD, K03385 nrfA, K15876 nrfH); polyphosphate (3 KOs: K00937 ppk, K22468 ppk2, K01514 ppx); phosphate transport/regulation (8 KOs: K02040 pstS, K02037 pstC, K02038 pstA, K02036 pstB, K02035 pit, K07657 phoB, K07636 phoR, K02039 phoU); polyhydroxyalkanoate (PHA) metabolism (4 KOs: K00626 phaA, K00023 phaB, K03821 phaC, K05973 phaZ); acetate activation (3 KOs: K00634 pta, K00925 ackA, K01895 acs); propionate/methylcitrate metabolism (5 KOs: K01659 prpC, K01720 prpD, K01908 prpE, K01847 mcmA, K01848 mcmB); and glycogen metabolism (5 KOs: K00703 glgC, K00700 glgA, K00702 glgB, K00688 glgP, K01214 glgX). DRAM was not run (`do_dram: false`); deep CAZy/MEROPS/AMR annotation is therefore not available for this dataset.

### 2.7 16S rRNA ASV-to-MAG linkage and iterativeID assignment

DADA2 representative ASV sequences (3,950 sequences from `rep_seqs_merged_dada2.qza`) were linked to MAGs by aligning them to MAG-borne 16S rRNA genes. Target 16S genes were extracted from the Bakta `.ffn` outputs (not from barrnap), yielding 343 16S sequences across 239 distinct MAGs (up to six copies per MAG). Alignment used minimap2 v2.30-r1287 in short-read mode retaining the single best hit (`minimap2 -ax sr -t 32 --secondary=no -N 1 mag_16s.fasta asvs.fasta`). Each ASV was assigned a confidence tier requiring both percent identity and query coverage: species (≥97% identity and ≥80% coverage), genus (≥95% and ≥70%), family (≥90% and ≥60%), weak (aligned but below family thresholds), or no_alignment. Of the 3,950 ASVs, 3,798 had a best MAG hit (species 1,339; genus 340; family 801; weak 1,318) and 152 had no alignment; the 2,480 confidence-tier ASVs (species+genus+family) constituted the pool used for amplicon-derived abundance. 236 MAGs were hit by at least one ASV at any tier, of which 232 were hit by at least one confidence-tier ASV.

A per-MAG "iterativeID" was assigned by inheriting an ASV iterativeID when the ASV was resolved to at least family level and its family matched the MAG's GTDB family (preferring genus-level matches, with ties broken by highest mean relative abundance), and otherwise minting a new identifier of the form `{GTDB_Genus}_m.N` (or `{GTDB_Family}_m.N` when genus was unknown). This produced 31 genus-level inherited, 82 family-level inherited, and 163 newly minted identifiers (276 total, no blanks). The ASV-level iterativeID system itself comprised 3,945 identifiers stored in `iterativeIDs.json`, `iterativeID_taxonomy.json` and `iterativeID_levels.json`. The 16S amplicon taxonomy used a MiDAS (SILVA-derived, wastewater-specific) reference, evidenced by placeholder labels such as `midas_g_*`/`midas_s_*`, and is therefore a distinct scheme from the GTDB taxonomy applied to the MAGs; the precise MiDAS release string was not recoverable from the QIIME2 artefact provenance.

### 2.8 MAG relative abundance: amplicon-derived and metagenome-derived

**Amplicon-derived (per day).** For each (MAG, day) cell, the per-day relative abundances of all confidence-tier ASVs whose best hit was that MAG were summed, across 75 sampling days spanning study days 1–584. Two variants of the resulting table were produced (`mag_abundance_by_day_intersection.csv` and `..._union.csv`); the per-day abundance matrices were numerically identical between the two (maximum absolute difference 0.0), differing only in their taxonomy-consensus columns (the intersection variant retaining a rank label only where all mapped, ≥order-resolved ASVs agreed; the union variant semicolon-joining all distinct labels per rank). Per-day column sums ranged 94.83–99.75% (not 100%, because only confidence-tier ASVs mapping to a MAG were summed). A read-mapping counterpart (`mag_16s_abundance.csv`, 239 MAGs × 75 days) was also produced by mapping the raw per-sample 16S amplicon reads to the 343-sequence 16S index with minimap2 (`-ax sr --secondary=no`) and normalising hit-counts per MAG so that each day column summed to exactly 100% (value range 0–35.48%), over the 239 MAGs carrying a called 16S gene.

**Metagenome-derived (CoverM).** The five samples' fastp-cleaned Illumina reads were competitively mapped to a single concatenated reference of all 276 MAGs (`run_mag_abundance.sh`). Each MAG's contigs were prefixed with `MAGname~` to make contig names globally unique, the reference was indexed with bwa-mem2 v2.2.1, and each sample was mapped with `bwa-mem2 mem -t 32` and sorted with samtools v1.23.1. Relative abundance was computed in a single CoverM v0.7.0 invocation in genome mode (`coverm genome --bam-files <5 BAMs> --separator '~' -m relative_abundance trimmed_mean covered_fraction --min-covered-fraction 0 --threads 16`), with contigs regrouped to genomes via the `~` separator. CoverM relative abundances (reported in percent, including an `unmapped` row) were divided by 100 to fractions and the `unmapped` row dropped. The fraction of reads assigned to MAGs ranged from 87.37% (CAN_1) to 92.84% (CAN_5; per-sample 87.37/87.84/88.54/91.80/92.84%), and 201–244 of the 276 MAGs were detected above 0.01% relative abundance per sample. Results were written to `mag_metagenome_abundance.csv` (276 × 5 fractions) and injected into the master summary table as `CAN_{1..5}_wgs_relabund` columns.

### 2.9 Inference of nitrogen- and phosphorus-cycling metabolic potential

Metabolic phenotypes were inferred from the 42-KO presence/absence matrix (read from the current `mag_abundance_summary.tsv`) by a deterministic rule set (`derive_pathways.py`), yielding 22 derived columns (9 module intermediates and 13 phenotype designations). Module intermediates were defined as Boolean combinations of KOs: `core_polyP_gene` = ppk OR ppk2 (K00937 | K22468); `phosphate_transport` = the complete pstSCAB complex OR pit ((K02040 & K02037 & K02038 & K02036) | K02035); `phosphate_regulation_support` = phoB OR phoR OR phoU; `PHA_module` = any of phaA/phaB/phaC/phaZ; `PHA_synthesis_module` = phaA AND phaB AND phaC; `glycogen_synthesis_module` = glgC AND glgA; `supportive_glycogen_genes` = glgB OR glgP OR glgX; `carbon_activation_module` = any of pta/ackA/acs/prpC/prpD/prpE/mcmA/mcmB; and `weak_absent_polyP` = NOT core_polyP_gene.

Denitrification phenotypes used internal helpers: nitrate reduction (`NO3_to_NO2`) = the narGHI complex (K00370 & K00371 & K00374) OR the napAB complex (K02567 & K02568); nitrite reduction (`NO2_to_NO`) = nirS OR nirK; nitric-oxide reduction = norB (K04561, the strong marker); nitrous-oxide reduction = nosZ (K00376); and an `upstream_denitri` term comprising every gene upstream of nosZ. `complete_denitrification` required each of the four steps to be present (NO3→NO2, NO2→NO, norB and nosZ simultaneously), i.e. a step-wise complete pathway rather than the presence of all ten denitrification KOs; `incomplete_denitrification` was any denitrification gene without the complete step-wise chain. Two N2O-sink phenotypes partitioned the nosZ-bearing MAGs: `N2O_sink_non_denitrifying` (nosZ with no upstream denitrification gene) and `N2O_sink_denitrifying` (nosZ plus ≥1 upstream gene). DNRA potential was inferred from nrfA (`respiratory_DNRA_potential` = K03385; the documented simplification of "K03385 OR (K03385 & K15876)"), and `possible_nitrite_ammonification` from nirB OR nirD.

PAO-like phenotypes formed a nested series of increasing stringency: `polyP_capable_PAO_unresolved` (core polyP gene), `stronger_polyP_phosphate_cycling` (+ phosphate transport), `putative_PAO_like` (+ PHA module), and `stronger_putative_PAO_like` (+ phosphate regulation support and the strict three-gene PHA synthesis module). GAO/storage phenotypes all required `weak_absent_polyP` (no ppk/ppk2), rendering them mutually exclusive of every PAO tier: `GAO_like` (glycogen synthesis + carbon activation + no polyP), `putative_GAO_like_storage` (additionally requiring supportive glycogen genes and a PHA module), and `PHA_capable_non_glycogen_storage` (PHA module without glycogen synthesis, no polyP). A hand-curated, literature-anchored genus roster (`gao_pao_categories.json`) listing canonical PAOs (*Ca.* Accumulibacter, Tetrasphaera, Dechloromonas, Microlunatus, Azonexus, *Ca.* Phosphoribacter), GAOs (*Ca.* Competibacter, Defluviicoccus, Propionivibrio, *Ca.* Contendobacter), putative PAOs and GAOs (e.g. *Thauera*, Zoogloea, Paracoccus, Micropruina, Amaricoccus), and other PHA-storing genera was retained for interpretation but was not used as input to the rule set. It is documented in the deriving script that nosZ (K00376) served as a clade I+II proxy only and could not, on its own, distinguish nosZ clades without downstream phylogenetics (§2.10).

### 2.10 Gene-resolved denitrification/DNRA abundance and nosZ clade typing

A gene-resolved analysis of denitrification and DNRA genes was performed on a focal set of MAGs (`gene_ab_figure/`). The focal set was defined by taking, for each of the five samples, the top ten CheckM2 high-quality MAGs (completeness ≥ 90% and contamination ≤ 5%) ranked by CoverM relative abundance, and forming the union of the five lists; this yielded 21 focal MAGs. Target gene coordinates were derived from Bakta GFF3 CDS features (with Bakta-renamed contigs mapped back to original assembly contigs via each MAG's Bakta JSON), restricted to the union of KOfamScan and eggNOG KO calls; nirB/nirD (K00362/K00363) were accepted at a relaxed threshold (55% of the official KOfam cutoff, with eggNOG corroboration) because the native cutoff was very strict. The resulting `target_genes.bed` contained 73 gene loci across 12 KOs.

Gene abundance was quantified as RPKM from Nanopore reads mapped to a 21-MAG focal reference (`combined_ref.fa`) rather than to the 276-MAG community reference. This deliberate scope choice was made because re-quantification against the 276-MAG community reference caused conserved denitrification genes (nirS, nosZ, norB, nirB) to lose 70–87% of their reads to homologues in non-focal MAGs (illustrated by *Desulfobacillus*/CAN_2_bin.6 nirS, whose originally committed count of 824 reproduced at 813 on the 21-MAG reference but collapsed to 52 on the 276-MAG reference). Re-counting against the 21-MAG focal reference reproduced the originally committed per-gene counts with a median ratio of 0.989; this is therefore a reproducibility check of the focal-reference counts, not a validation of absolute abundance against an external gold standard. Per-gene read counts were obtained with `samtools view -c -F 4` over each BED interval, and RPKM was computed as count / gene_length_kb / total_mapped_million, where per-sample total mapped reads were CAN_1 6,642,990; CAN_2 5,048,250; CAN_3 7,353,164; CAN_4 9,264,691; and CAN_5 12,334,535. Each figure-row cell was the maximum RPKM over the subunit genes of that row. Because the reads are Nanopore long reads and any read overlapping a gene interval is counted, a single long read may span more than one gene; the resulting RPKM values are therefore best interpreted as a relative, within-figure comparator and are not directly comparable to conventional short-read RPKM, and cross-gene magnitude comparisons are semi-quantitative.

The claded RPKM matrix (`gene_rpkm_per_sample_claded.tsv`) comprised 8 functional rows (nar, nap, nirB/D, nirS, nirK, norC, nosZ_cladeI, nosZ_cladeII) across the 19 of the 21 MAGs carrying ≥1 target gene (the two "Other"-classified MAGs, CAN_1_bin.174 and CAN_5_bin.90, carried no target gene and were excluded from the RPKM matrix). Of these rows, the "nar" row was the maximum RPKM over narG/narH/narI (K00370/K00371/K00374), the "nap" row over napA/napB (K02567/K02568), the "nirB/D" row over nirB/nirD (K00362/K00363), and — importantly — the "norC" row was the maximum RPKM over both norC (K02305) and norB (K04561); because norB is the more abundant gene (11 loci versus 4) and the stronger marker, the "norC" row in most MAGs principally reflects the norBC nitric-oxide-reductase signal rather than a norC-specific signal.

nosZ clade typing operated independently on assembled gene sequences. The 13 nosZ CDS were extracted from the focal reference and scored with nhmmer (via pyhmmer v0.12.1) against eight nucleotide HMMs built from curated reference alignments — one Clade I reference (NosZREF-CladeI, 157 sequences) and seven Clade II sub-clade references (NosZREF-1577 sub-clades II.A–II.H, excluding II.F; Clade II total 374 sequences). A gene was called Clade I if its Clade-I bit-score met or exceeded the maximum of the seven Clade-II sub-clade scores, and Clade II otherwise; confidence was "high" when the margin was ≥40 bits and "low" otherwise. Clade II genes were then assigned to a sub-clade by retaining the highest-scoring Clade-II sub-clade, with confidence high (≥40 bits margin to runner-up), medium (≥10 bits), or low (<10 bits). Clade typing and RPKM quantification were independent (the former operating on assembled sequence via nhmmer, the latter on read mapping).

A complementary copy-number analysis produced two products: a read-coverage-based copy number (gene depth normalised by whole-genome depth via `samtools idxstats` on the 21-MAG BAMs, per-group maximum; `gene_copy_number.tsv`, four KO groups across 19 MAGs) and a reference-independent genomic copy inventory (distinct gene loci per MAG and KO counted directly from `target_genes.bed`, with nosZ split by clade; `gene_copy_per_mag.tsv`, all 21 MAGs).

### 2.11 Data processing, tables, and figure generation

Tabular analyses were performed in Python (pandas 2.3.3, Python 3.11.15). The master summary table `mag_abundance_summary.tsv` (276 rows × 228 data columns plus the `MAG` index) was assembled across several stages and comprised: 7 GTDB taxonomy ranks (Domain–Species); 2 iterative-ID columns (`iterativeIDs`, `mag_iterativeID`); 42 KO presence/absence columns; 22 derived pathway/phenotype columns; 150 interleaved 16S time-series columns (75 days × {`_asv_sum`, `_read_sum`}); and 5 metagenome relative-abundance columns (`CAN_{1..5}_wgs_relabund`). A companion JSON (`mag_abundance_summary.json`) stored the same data keyed by MAG with sub-keys `taxonomy`, `abundance`, `iterativeIDs`, `ko_presence`, `wgs_relabund`, `wgs_trimmed_mean`, `wgs_covered_fraction` and `pathway_modules`. Cross-file integrity was verified: the `day_N_asv_sum` columns matched the intersection-CSV `day_N` values (maximum absolute difference 4.4×10⁻¹⁶); the `day_N_read_sum` columns matched `mag_16s_abundance.csv` (difference 0.0); and the intersection and union by-day day-values were identical (difference 0.0).

Assembly statistics (Table 2) were computed with seqkit v2.13.0 (`seqkit stats -a -T`) directly on the final assemblies, as the workflow's `stats` rule and `publish` rule had not been executed. The KO matrices (`ko_presence_absence_matrix.csv`, 276 × 42; `ko_copy_number_matrix.csv`, 276 × 42; `ko_hits_long.csv`, 8,088 rows; `ko_pathway_summary.csv`, 276 × 41) were produced by the KOfam/eggNOG union post-processing. The gene-abundance heatmap (`gene_abundance_figure.png`/`.pdf`) was rendered from `gene_rpkm_per_sample_claded.tsv` (8 N-transformation row groups; columns = 19 MAGs labelled by iterativeID and subdivided by the five samples; a GAO/PAO/denitrifier colour bar; open circles marking low-confidence clade calls), and the copy-number figure (`gene_copy_number_figure.png`/`.pdf`) from the coverage-based copy-number tables.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Assembly statistics

The hybrid long-read-anchored track produced substantially more contiguous assemblies than the short-read track (Table 2). The five final hybrid per-sample assemblies ranged from 18,097 to 28,543 contigs and from 478.2 to 643.8 Mbp in total length, with contig N50 values of 39,954–90,597 bp and largest contigs of 5.41–6.16 Mbp (GC 57.82–59.36%); the recovery of contigs in the 5–6.2 Mbp range is consistent with near-complete single bacterial genomes recovered on the hybrid path. The single five-sample hybrid co-assembly comprised 54,369 contigs totalling 1,639,619,559 bp (≈1.64 Gbp) with a contig N50 of 101,778 bp, a largest contig of 6,168,456 bp, and a GC content of 58.36% (verified independently by `seqkit stats -a -T`).

By contrast, the short-read metaSPAdes assemblies were far more fragmented, as expected. Per-sample short-read assemblies comprised 566,479–1,099,769 contigs totalling 545.5–834.6 Mbp, with contig N50 values of only ~1.0–1.8 kb (986–1,756 bp; lowest 986 bp in CAN_3) and largest contigs of 558,196–2,108,850 bp (GC 58.62–60.22%); the short-read co-assembly comprised 2,112,358 contigs totalling 1,959,307,627 bp with a contig N50 of 1,657 bp and a largest contig of 2,737,980 bp. The hybrid track thus improved contig N50 by roughly one to two orders of magnitude relative to short-read assembly, and it was the hybrid assemblies that supported all downstream binning.

### 3.2 MAG recovery, dereplication and quality

MetaBAT2 binning of the hybrid assemblies produced 1,623 candidate bins (211/177/230/243/182 from the per-sample assemblies and 580 from the co-assembly). After CheckM2 assessment and dRep dereplication (completeness ≥ 50%, contamination ≤ 10%, 95% ANI), 651 candidate bins passed the quality pre-filter and clustered into 276 species-level clusters, yielding a final catalogue of 276 dereplicated MAGs (confirmed across two independent directories and the dRep `Widb.csv` winner table). Every one of the 276 MAGs met the medium-quality floor; 162 met the high-quality completeness/contamination proxy (≥90% completeness, ≤5% contamination). Across the 276 MAGs, median completeness was 95.44% (mean 89.03%, range 50.04–100.00%) and median contamination was 1.00% (mean 1.63%, range 0.00–8.83%); 171 MAGs were ≥90% complete, 74 fell in [70,90)% and 31 in [50,70)%. MAG genome sizes averaged 3.68 Mbp (median 3.60 Mbp, range 0.50–9.76 Mbp) with a mean contig N50 of 973,356 bp (median 226,590 bp). It is emphasised that the high/medium-quality designations rest on CheckM2 completeness and contamination only and do not incorporate the rRNA/tRNA criteria of the full MIMAG standard.

### 3.3 Taxonomy of the MAG catalogue

GTDB-Tk (r220) classified all 276 MAGs as Bacteria (no Archaea, no failed genomes), spanning 14 phyla. The catalogue was dominated by Pseudomonadota (122 MAGs), Bacteroidota (59), and Chloroflexota (34), which together accounted for 215 of 276 MAGs (78%); the remaining phyla were Patescibacteria (21), Planctomycetota (10), Verrucomicrobiota (9), Acidobacteriota (8), Spirochaetota (4), Myxococcota (3), Bdellovibrionota (2), and one MAG each of Gemmatimonadota, Hydrogenedentota, Armatimonadota and Elusimicrobiota. Rank resolution was complete through order (276/276), with 275 MAGs resolved at family, 260 at genus (171 distinct genera), and 92 at species (184 unnamed). The high genus-level but low species-level resolution, together with the 44 MAGs flagged as taxonomic novelty by RED, is typical of GTDB-Tk placement of environmental MAGs.

### 3.4 Functional repertoire and the target N/P gene panel

Bakta annotation of the 276 MAGs (≈1.02 Gbp across 14,633 contigs) predicted 930,591 CDSs (of which 253,014 were hypothetical), 11,966 tRNAs, 228 tmRNAs, 998 rRNAs, 1,455 ncRNAs, 1,775 ncRNA regions, 10,466 pseudogenes, 42 sORFs, and 400 CRISPR arrays. The curated proteome comprised 930,633 protein records (930,591 CDSs + 42 sORFs).

For the 42-KO N/P panel, the KOfam HMM and eggNOG calls were combined as a union. In the standalone `ko_presence_absence_matrix.csv` snapshot, this union recovered 5,137 MAG×KO pairs in total — 4,329 from the KOfam HMM scan (across 267 MAGs) and 4,777 from eggNOG (across 264 MAGs); the KOfam HMM scan added 360 MAG×KO calls beyond eggNOG across 106 MAGs, including all 9 MAGs for which eggNOG produced empty output. The largest fractional KOfam-only rescues were for prpC (+32), pit (+24), ppk2 (+22), phoR (+17) and glgX (+16). The protein-level union (`ko_hits_long.csv`) comprised 8,088 hits over 7,984 unique proteins spanning 264 MAGs and all 42 KOs.

Per-KO prevalences reported below were computed from the current `mag_abundance_summary.tsv` (the matrix that feeds the phenotype inference of §3.5) so that the prevalence and phenotype counts are mutually consistent. Among individual KOs, the phosphate and storage machinery was near-ubiquitous: phaA (236/276), phoR (233), pstS (229), acs (231), and ppk (227) were the most prevalent, whereas ppx (K01514) was effectively absent, detected in only a single MAG. Denitrification-gene prevalence was led by nosZ (123/276), norB (119), nirK (99), norC (89), and nirS (80), with the upstream nitrate-reduction genes narG/narH/narI present in 54–56 MAGs and napA/napB in 46–49. DNRA markers nrfA and nrfH were each present in 63 MAGs, and nirB and nirD in 66 and 81 MAGs respectively. At the module level, no MAG carried a complete 10-gene denitrification chain and none carried all three polyphosphate genes; phosphate transport/regulation was the most complete module (80/276 MAGs with all eight phosphate genes; 258/276 with ≥1), followed by PHA synthesis (74 MAGs complete; 235 with ≥1) and acetate activation (50 complete; 228 with ≥1). The full per-pathway completeness summary (`ko_pathway_summary.csv`), giving for each of the eight pathways the number of MAGs that were complete, the number carrying ≥1 KO, and the mean per-pathway completeness, is given in Table 3: denitrification (0 complete / 218 with ≥1 / mean 25.9%), DNRA/nitrogen-other (4 / 128 / 24.4%), polyphosphate (0 / 228 / 41.4%), phosphate (80 / 258 / 66.3%), PHA (74 / 235 / 51.2%), acetate (50 / 228 / 47.1%), propionate/methylcitrate (3 / 228 / 30.7%), and glycogen (15 / 198 / 44.1%).

### 3.5 N/P metabolic-phenotype inference

Phenotype inference across the 276 MAGs (from `derive_pathways.py`, computed on the current table) revealed extensive but largely incomplete denitrification potential and widespread phosphorus/storage capacity. Of the module intermediates, `carbon_activation_module` was the most prevalent (256/276, dominated by acs at 231), followed by `phosphate_regulation_support` (249), `phosphate_transport` (244), `PHA_module` (243), and `core_polyP_gene` (240); `weak_absent_polyP` (the exact complement of core polyP) was present in 36 MAGs. The strict three-gene PHA synthesis module was present in 117 MAGs, the glycogen synthesis module in 133, and the supportive glycogen genes in 179.

Among the 13 phenotype designations, `incomplete_denitrification` (205/276) outnumbered `complete_denitrification` (28/276) by approximately seven to one. The two phenotypes together (233 MAGs) define the set carrying at least one denitrification gene, since `incomplete_denitrification` is defined as any denitrification gene without the complete step-wise chain; thus 233 of 276 MAGs encoded at least one denitrification gene. nosZ-bearing MAGs numbered 123 and partitioned exactly into `N2O_sink_denitrifying` (95, nosZ with ≥1 upstream gene) and `N2O_sink_non_denitrifying` (28, nosZ without any upstream gene). DNRA potential (`respiratory_DNRA_potential`, nrfA) was inferred in 63 MAGs, and `possible_nitrite_ammonification` (nirB/nirD) in 91. The PAO-like phenotypes formed the intended nested series of decreasing prevalence: `polyP_capable_PAO_unresolved` 240 → `stronger_polyP_phosphate_cycling` 216 → `putative_PAO_like` 206 → `stronger_putative_PAO_like` 105. By contrast, the GAO/storage phenotypes — which require absence of polyP — were rare: `GAO_like` (4 MAGs), `putative_GAO_like_storage` (2), and `PHA_capable_non_glycogen_storage` (14).

### 3.6 ASV-to-MAG linkage outcome

Linkage of the 3,950 DADA2 ASVs to the 343 Bakta-called 16S genes (spanning 239 MAGs) assigned 3,798 ASVs to a best MAG hit and left 152 unaligned; by confidence tier, 1,339 ASVs were species-level, 340 genus-level, 801 family-level and 1,318 weak. The 2,480 confidence-tier ASVs linked to MAGs underpinned the amplicon-derived abundance: 236 MAGs were hit by ≥1 ASV at any tier, but only 232 received nonzero amplicon abundance (confidence-tier ASVs only), and the remaining 44 MAGs were taxonomy-blank in the by-day tables. The per-MAG iterativeID assignment produced 31 genus-level inherited, 82 family-level inherited, and 163 newly minted identifiers.

### 3.7 Amplicon- versus metagenome-derived abundance and dominant taxa

The amplicon (by-day) and metagenome (CoverM) views were structurally distinct: the by-day tables spanned 75 sampling days (study days 1–584) with per-day confidence-tier ASV sums of 94.83–99.75%, whereas the CoverM view spanned the five metagenome samples with 87.37–92.84% of reads assignable to the MAG catalogue (244, 238, 244, 234 and 201 MAGs detected above 0.01% in CAN_1–CAN_5 respectively). The intersection and union by-day variants carried identical abundance values but differed sharply in taxonomy coverage (non-blank phylum 130 vs 232; non-blank genus 54 vs 231), the union variant including semicolon-joined multi-source labels that inflate its populated counts relative to the strict single-label intersection variant. These by-day taxonomy-consensus labels derive from the MiDAS ASV scheme, not the GTDB MAG taxonomy.

The metagenome-derived ranking (`mag_metagenome_abundance.csv`) was dominated by canonical EBPR functional guilds, named here in GTDB taxonomy. The single most abundant MAG was CAN_5_bin.106, a *Competibacter phosphatis* (a canonical glycogen-accumulating organism, GAO), at a mean relative abundance of 0.1967 (up to 0.272 in CAN_3). Two of the top four MAGs were *Candidatus* Accumulibacter (canonical polyphosphate-accumulating organisms, PAOs): coasm_bin.185 (*Accumulibacter* sp000585075, mean 0.0607, peaking at 0.189 in CAN_5) and CAN_1_bin.98 (*Accumulibacter* sp014879755, mean 0.0479, peaking at 0.114 in CAN_4). A *Thauera* denitrifier (CAN_4_bin.236, *Thauera* sp017997795) ranked third (mean 0.0596), and a second *Competibacter* (CAN_4_bin.116) ranked tenth. The co-occurrence of dominant GAO and PAO populations together with abundant denitrifiers situates this community within the EBPR functional paradigm.

### 3.8 Gene-resolved denitrification and nosZ clade/sub-clade findings

The gene-resolved RPKM heatmap (`gene_abundance_figure.png`) over the 19 focal MAGs carrying target genes revealed a partitioned, taxon-specific distribution of denitrification steps. Only 3 MAGs carried a non-zero `nar` (membrane nitrate reductase) signal (CAN_5_bin.106, CAN_5_bin.40, coasm_bin.55), while 6 carried `nap` (periplasmic nitrate reductase). Nitrite reduction was split between the cytochrome cd1 (`nirS`, 9 MAGs) and copper (`nirK`, 2 MAGs) enzymes, with little overlap. The GAO CAN_5_bin.106 (*Ca.* Competibacter.139) dominated the `norC` row, which captures the maximum of the norB and norC signal (up to 1,079.7 RPKM in CAN_3), and `nar` (up to 328.3), while the PAO coasm_bin.185 (*Ca.* Accumulibacter.8) peaked in CAN_5 for `nap` (289.1), nirB/D (137.0), nirS (148.0) and nosZ Clade II (132.8). Given the long-read RPKM caveat (§2.10), these between-gene magnitudes should be read as semi-quantitative.

Of the 13 assembled nosZ genes typed from the focal set, 12 were called Clade II and only 1 Clade I; the single Clade I call (coasm_bin.55, *Competibacter*) was itself a low-confidence near-tie (margin +7.9 bits, 195.4 vs 187.5), so it is more accurately described as an unresolved Clade I/II near-tie, and the only other low-confidence call (CAN_5_bin.40, *Giesbergeria*, margin −24.2 bits) was Clade II. Within Clade II, sub-clade typing of the 12 genes yielded 7× II.D, 2× II.G, 2× II.C and 1× II.B. The II.D sub-clade was dominant, comprising both *Accumulibacter* genes plus *Desulfobacillus*/Denitratisoma, *Azonexus*, JAEULV01, JAAEKA01 and *Leptovillus*, with high confidence for five and medium confidence for two (coasm_bin.260, coasm_bin.481). The three Bacteroidota genes (OLB5 → II.B; JJ008 and SpSt-398 → II.C) sat at the II.B/II.C boundary with margins of only 0.8–2.2 bits and are best reported as Clade II Bacteroidota (II.B/C). The reference-independent genomic copy inventory (`gene_copy_per_mag.tsv`) summed to 73 loci across the 21 MAGs (column totals: narG 3, narH 3, narI 2, napA 6, napB 6, nirB 9, nirD 1, nirS 13, nirK 2, norB 11, norC 4, nosZ Clade I 1, nosZ Clade II 12), with the highest-inventory MAGs being *Giesbergeria* (CAN_5_bin.40, 9 genes) and *Competibacter* (coasm_bin.55, 8 genes).

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 N/P-cycling potential in EBPR context

The CAN community displayed the metabolic signatures expected of an EBPR-type bioreactor while resolving the underlying genomic structure at MAG and gene level. Denitrification potential was pervasive but modular: 233 MAGs carried at least one denitrification gene and 205 were classified as incomplete denitrifiers, yet only 28 (10% of the catalogue) carried a complete step-wise narGHI/napAB → nirS/nirK → norB → nosZ pathway, and no single MAG carried all ten denitrification KOs. Complete step-wise denitrification potential was therefore present in a minority of MAGs, while the broader community pattern was dominated by partial pathways — consistent with metabolic hand-off among taxa for the fraction of nitrogen processed by incomplete denitrifiers, a pattern widely reported for activated-sludge and EBPR systems. DNRA potential was a substantial minority capacity (nrfA in 63 MAGs), indicating a competing nitrite-fate pathway that retains nitrogen as ammonium rather than reducing it to gas; the coexistence of denitrification and DNRA markers across distinct MAGs is consistent with niche partitioning under fluctuating carbon-to-nitrogen conditions.

The nosZ findings are notable for nitrous-oxide budgeting. By the 42-KO presence/absence analysis, nosZ was the single most prevalent denitrification gene (123 of 276 MAGs), and 95 of these co-encoded upstream denitrification genes while 28 carried nosZ without any upstream gene — the latter representing potential non-denitrifying N2O sinks. Explicit phylogenetic typing was performed only on the 13 nosZ genes assembled from the 21-MAG focal set (approximately 11% of the ~123 community nosZ genes, drawn from the most abundant MAGs); 12 of these 13 focal genes typed as Clade II and 1 was an unresolved Clade I/II near-tie. Within this focal sample, Clade II ("atypical") nosZ — which is enriched in organisms that act as N2O sinks, often lacking a complete upstream denitrification pathway — therefore predominated. The community-wide Clade I/Clade II split was not determined, because the master-table nosZ (K00376) column is an unclade-able proxy and only the focal genes were phylogenetically typed; the genomic potential for N2O reduction described here is thus bounded to the abundant focal populations and should not be generalised to the whole community without broader typing. Of note, both *Ca.* Accumulibacter MAGs in the focal set carried a Clade II nosZ (sub-clade II.D), indicating that at least some PAO-affiliated populations encode N2O-reduction potential. As genomic potential for N2O reduction is not equivalent to net N2O consumption (which depends on expression, oxygen, kinetics, and the balance against N2O-producing steps), all such statements refer to genomic capacity rather than measured flux.

The phosphorus and storage phenotypes were consistent with a polyphosphate-cycling community. Core polyP capacity (ppk/ppk2) was present in 240 MAGs and phosphate transport in 244, and the nested PAO-like tiers (240 → 216 → 206 → 105) indicate that while polyP and phosphate-transport genes are very broadly distributed, the stricter combination of polyP, phosphate transport, phosphate regulation and complete PHA synthesis (105 MAGs) defines a more selective putative-PAO core. The metagenome abundance data placed canonical PAOs (*Ca.* Accumulibacter, two of the top four MAGs) and a canonical GAO (*Competibacter phosphatis*, the single most abundant MAG) at the top of the community — the classic PAO–GAO competition that governs EBPR performance. The rarity of the strict KO-based GAO_like phenotype (4 MAGs) despite the numerical dominance of a *Competibacter* GAO highlights that storage-based lifestyles are defined as much by regulation and ecophysiology as by the presence/absence of individual KOs, and that the conservative `weak_absent_polyP` requirement excludes genuine storage organisms that nonetheless retain a ppk/ppk2 gene. Notably, the dominant *Competibacter* GAO (CAN_5_bin.106) encoded substantial denitrification machinery, dominating the norBC and nar RPKM rows, and a second *Competibacter* (coasm_bin.55) carried the sole focal-set Clade I nosZ call; this denitrifying-GAO phenotype is ecologically relevant to N2O dynamics in EBPR systems and merits targeted follow-up.

### 4.2 Value of hybrid assembly

The hybrid long-read-anchored pipeline delivered MAG-quality and 16S-recovery advantages that a short-read-only approach could not. Hybrid contig N50 values (40–102 kb across per-sample and co-assembly) exceeded the short-read metaSPAdes N50 values (~1–1.8 kb) by one to two orders of magnitude, and the hybrid assemblies recovered numerous 5–6.2 Mbp contigs approaching complete bacterial chromosomes. This contiguity translated directly into MAG quality (median completeness 95.44%, median contamination 1.00%, 162/276 high-quality) and, critically, into the recovery of full-length 16S rRNA genes within MAGs — 343 16S genes across 239 of the 276 MAGs — which was the prerequisite for direct ASV-to-MAG linkage. Short-read assemblies, which fragment the rRNA operon and produced zero usable bins in this run, would not have supported the amplicon-to-genome bridge that connects the 75-day 16S time-series to the genome-resolved functional inferences.

### 4.3 Methodological strengths and caveats

Several methodological points qualify the interpretation. First, the gene-resolved RPKM analysis was deliberately computed against a 21-MAG focal reference rather than the full 276-MAG community, because conserved denitrification genes lost 70–87% of their reads to homologues on the community reference (read leakage); re-counting against the 21-MAG reference reproduced the committed counts at a median ratio of 0.989. This makes the focal RPKM values internally consistent but means they should not be read as community-wide gene abundances, and the gene figure denominator is 19 MAGs (those carrying ≥1 target gene), not the 21 focal MAGs nor the 276-MAG catalogue. Moreover, because RPKM was computed from Nanopore long reads counted as any-overlap over short CDS intervals, the values are a relative within-figure comparator and cross-gene magnitude comparisons are semi-quantitative.

Second, the master summary table uses nosZ (K00376) as a clade proxy and cannot distinguish nosZ Clade I from Clade II; the explicit clade resolution comes only from the separate nhmmer-based typing (`nosz_clades.tsv`, `nosz_cladeII_subclades.tsv`) on the focal MAGs, so any clade interpretation drawn from the summary table's K00376 column should be regarded as a conservative proxy, and the focal clade typing covers only 13 of the ~123 community nosZ genes.

Third, the Bacteroidota Clade II sub-clade assignments (II.B vs II.C) are ambiguous (margins 0.8–2.2 bits) and are best reported jointly as II.B/C; the II.D and II.G assignments are robust. Fourth, MAG quality rests on CheckM2 v1.1.0 completeness/contamination rather than CheckM, and the MIMAG tiers are proxies that do not incorporate rRNA/tRNA criteria — a relevant caveat given that not all MAGs carry a detectable 16S gene (239/276). The near-absence of ppx (K01514, one MAG) should likewise be read with caution: it may reflect a strict targeted-HMM threshold or annotation limitation against divergent ppx sequences rather than genuine community-wide absence.

Fifth, the ASV-to-MAG linkage and the amplicon taxonomy-consensus rules introduce their own limits: only 232 MAGs received nonzero amplicon abundance, the intersection-versus-union taxonomy semantics differ substantially in coverage, and the 16S amplicon side used a MiDAS reference scheme distinct from the MAG GTDB taxonomy, complicating any direct cross-scheme reconciliation. Finally, the GTDB taxonomy is r220 although the GTDB-Tk binary (v2.7.2) natively expects r232; the runs completed without failure, but all GTDB labels should be cited as r220.

### 4.4 Conclusion

Hybrid Nanopore-plus-Illumina assembly of five CAN bioreactor metagenomes recovered a high-quality, genome-resolved catalogue of 276 bacterial MAGs that captures the canonical EBPR functional architecture: dominant PAO (*Ca.* Accumulibacter) and GAO (*Competibacter phosphatis*) populations, abundant and taxonomically partitioned denitrifiers, and a denitrification network in which step-wise completeness was confined to a minority of MAGs (28/276). Among the abundant focal populations whose nosZ genes were phylogenetically typed, Clade II nosZ predominated — including within the PAO guild — pointing to genomic potential as a nitrous-oxide sink in those populations, alongside a DNRA-capable minority that offers an alternative nitrite fate. The recovery of full-length 16S genes within MAGs enabled a direct bridge from a 75-day amplicon time-series to genome-resolved N- and P-cycling potential, demonstrating the analytical value of hybrid assembly for engineered-community metagenomics while underscoring the importance of focal-reference scoping and explicit phylogenetic typing for gene-resolved nitrogen-cycle inference.

The two files carry identical day-abundance values (maximum difference 0.0) and differ only in taxonomy-consensus columns: intersection (strict single-label agreement) versus union (semicolon-joined multi-source MiDAS labels). Per-day column sums range 94.83–99.75%.

**mag_16s_abundance.csv — Read-mapping 16S amplicon abundance.** 239 MAGs (those carrying a Bakta-called 16S gene) × 75 days; each (MAG, day) cell is the percentage of raw per-sample 16S amplicon reads assigned to that MAG's 16S gene(s) by minimap2 short-read mapping to the 343-sequence 16S index, normalised so that each day column sums to exactly 100% (value range 0–35.48%). This is the read-count-based counterpart to the ASV-sum amplicon abundance and appears in the master summary as the `day_N_read_sum` columns.

**asv_to_mag_mapping.json — ASV-to-MAG linkage.** Per-ASV records (3,950 ASVs) of best MAG hit, 16S gene id, percent identity, query coverage, alignment length, and confidence tier (species/genus/family/weak/no_alignment) from minimap2 v2.30 alignment of DADA2 rep-seqs to 343 Bakta-called MAG 16S genes, plus a metadata block with tier thresholds and summary counts (3,798 with a best hit; 152 unaligned).

**ko_presence_absence_matrix.csv / ko_copy_number_matrix.csv / ko_hits_long.csv / ko_pathway_summary.csv — Targeted KO results.** Binary presence (276 × 42) and copy-number (276 × 42) matrices for the 42-KO N/P panel; the long-format hit table (8,088 protein-level hits over 7,984 unique proteins across 264 MAGs and all 42 KOs) with MAG, protein_id, KO, gene, pathway and step; and a per-pathway completeness summary (276 × 41; see Table 3). All derive from the KOfam-HMM ∪ eggNOG union. Note that per-KO prevalences read from this standalone matrix differ by a few MAGs from those read from the regenerated `mag_abundance_summary.tsv`; the latter is canonical for the prevalence and phenotype counts in the text.

**gene_abundance_figure.png — Gene-resolved denitrification/DNRA RPKM heatmap.** Eight N-transformation row groups (nar, nap, nirB/D, nirS, nirK, norC [= max of norB and norC], nosZ Clade I, nosZ Clade II) over the 19 focal MAGs carrying ≥1 target gene; columns labelled by iterativeID and subdivided by the five samples; per-cell value is the maximum RPKM over a row's subunit genes (Nanopore reads mapped to the 21-MAG focal reference; semi-quantitative cross-gene comparison); a GAO/PAO/denitrifier colour bar annotates MAG class and open circles mark low-confidence clade calls; grey denotes gene absence.

**gene_copy_number_figure.png — Gene copy-number heatmap.** Read-coverage-based copy number (gene depth normalised to whole-genome depth, per-group maximum) for four KO groups (nirB/D, nirS, nirK, norBC/nosZ — here nosZ is merged with norBC and not clade-split) across 19 focal MAGs, from `samtools idxstats` on the 21-MAG BAMs (`gene_copy_number.tsv`). This coverage-based product is distinct from the reference-independent genomic copy inventory (`gene_copy_per_mag.tsv`, all 21 MAGs), which counts distinct loci directly from `target_genes.bed`.

**nosz_clades.tsv — nosZ Clade I/II typing.** For each of the 13 focal nosZ genes: MAG, gene id, genus, length, clade call (12 Clade II, 1 Clade I), confidence (11 high, 2 low), Clade-I and best Clade-II bit-scores, best sub-clade, and margin. Produced by nhmmer (pyhmmer v0.12.1) against eight reference nucleotide HMMs. The single Clade I call (coasm_bin.55) is a +7.9-bit near-tie and is unresolved between Clade I and II.

**nosz_cladeII_subclades.tsv — nosZ Clade II sub-clade assignment.** For the 12 Clade II genes: best sub-clade (7× II.D, 2× II.G, 2× II.C, 1× II.B), score, runner-up sub-clade and score, margin, and confidence (6 high, 3 medium, 3 low); the three Bacteroidota genes (II.B/II.C boundary, margins 0.8–2.2 bits) are flagged as ambiguous and best reported as II.B/C.

## 6. Data and code availability

The hybrid metagenomics workflow is implemented in Snakemake at `/scratch/afreiburger/metag-hybrid/pipeline/Snakefile`, with per-step conda environment specifications under `/scratch/afreiburger/metag-hybrid/pipeline/envs/*.yaml` and the run configuration at `/scratch1/afreiburger/emilykin/processed/config.yaml`. Processed pipeline outputs (assemblies, binning, CheckM2, dRep, Bakta, eggNOG, GTDB-Tk, CoverM abundance, and staged bins) reside under `/scratch1/afreiburger/emilykin/processed/`, and the KOfamScan targeted-KO workdir (`all_mags.faa`, `target_profiles.hmm`, `target_ko_list.txt`, `hmmsearch.tblout`, `postprocess.py`) under `/scratch1/afreiburger/emilykin/kofamscan/`. All analysis tables, JSON artefacts, the dereplicated genomes, the gene-abundance figure pipeline (`gene_ab_figure/`) and the QIIME2 16S artefacts (`qiime/`) are collected in the analysis repository at `/scratch1/afreiburger/emilykin/EmilyKin/`. KBase import artefacts (`kbase_mag_import.csv`, `kbase_genome_import.csv`) accompany the MAG catalogue for downstream import; the workflow's `publish` rule and `stats/all_assemblies.tsv` were not executed in this run, so all final outputs reside under the project-root tree rather than a separate publish directory, and the assembly statistics in Table 2 were computed fresh with seqkit v2.13.0. The paths above are internal compute-cluster locations; for publication, raw reads and assemblies will be deposited in a public sequence archive (NCBI/ENA) and the analysis tables in a DOI-assigning repository, with accession numbers to be provided.
